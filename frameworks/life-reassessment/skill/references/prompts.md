# The Verbatim Prompts

These are the exact questions from Dan Koe's protocol. **Use them word-for-word.**
The specific phrasing is doing deliberate psychological work — paraphrasing
softens it. Present **one at a time**, wait for a real answer, reflect, advance.

Coaching mode by part (see SKILL.md): **Part 1 and Part 2 = Socratic, never
answer for the user. Part 3 = active synthesis, help compress their material.**

---

## Part 1 — Morning: Psychological Excavation (Vision & Anti-Vision)

Frame for the user before starting: *"First we're creating a new lens for your
mind to operate from — like growing into a new shell. It won't fit at first.
That's the point. Answer from yourself; don't outsource this to me. If a question
won't come, we'll park it and come back."*

### 1a. Surfacing the dissatisfaction (awareness of current pain)

1. What is the dull and persistent dissatisfaction you've learned to live with?
   Not the deep suffering but what you've learned to tolerate. (If you don't hate
   it, you will tolerate it.)
2. What do you complain about repeatedly but never actually change? Write down the
   three complaints you've voiced most often in the past year.
3. For each complaint: What would someone who watched your behavior (not your
   words) conclude that you actually want?
4. What truth about your current life would be unbearable to admit to someone you
   deeply respect?

### 1b. The Anti-Vision (a brutal picture of the life you do NOT want)

Frame: *"Now we turn that pain into an 'anti-vision' — a vivid, brutal awareness
of the life you refuse to live, so you can use that energy to aim in a positive
direction."*

5. If absolutely nothing changes for the next five years, describe an average
   Tuesday. Where do you wake up? What does your body feel like? What's the first
   thing you think about? Who's around you? What do you do between 9am and 6pm?
   How do you feel at 10pm?
6. Now do it but for ten years. What have you missed? What opportunities closed?
   Who gave up on you? What do people say about you when you're not in the room?
7. You're at the end of your life. You lived the safe version. You never broke the
   pattern. What was the cost? What did you never let yourself feel, try, or
   become?
8. Who in your life is already living the future you just described? Someone five,
   ten, twenty years ahead on the same trajectory? What do you feel when you think
   about becoming them?
9. What identity would you have to give up to actually change? ("I am the type of
   person who...") What would it cost you socially to no longer be that person?
10. What is the most embarrassing reason you haven't changed? The one that makes
    you sound weak, scared, or lazy rather than reasonable?
11. If your current behavior is a form of self-protection, what exactly are you
    protecting? And what is that protection costing you?

(These last few are the ones most likely to get a cover-story answer. Probe once
for the real, embarrassing version — then let it land.)

### 1c. The Minimum Viable Vision (the life you DO want)

Frame: *"Now we orient that energy positively. Your vision is like a product — it
starts unclear and sharpens with time. We just need a minimum viable version."*

12. Forget practicality for a minute. If you could snap your fingers and be living
    a different life in three years — not what's realistic, what you actually want
    — what does an average Tuesday look like? Same level of detail as question 5.
13. What would you have to believe about yourself for that life to feel natural
    rather than forced? Write the identity statement: "I am the type of person
    who..."
14. What is one thing you would do this week if you were already that person?

## Part 2 — Throughout the Day: Interrupting Autopilot

Frame: *"Journaling alone won't change you — you have to break the unconscious
patterns running on autopilot. These questions are pattern-interrupts. Best
scheduled at random, non-conflicting moments — ideally while commuting, walking,
or lying around."*

### Timed interrupts (use these exact times and questions for reminders)

- **11:00am** — What am I avoiding right now by doing what I'm doing?
- **1:30pm** — If someone filmed the last two hours, what would they conclude I
  want from my life?
- **3:15pm** — Am I moving toward the life I hate or the life I want?
- **5:00pm** — What's the most important thing I'm pretending isn't important?
- **7:30pm** — What did I do today out of identity protection rather than genuine
  desire? (Hint: it's most things you do.)
- **9:00pm** — When did I feel most alive today? When did I feel most dead?

### Downtime questions (schedule during commuting / walking / lying around)

- What would change if I stopped needing people to see me as [the identity you
  wrote in question 10]?
- Where in my life am I trading aliveness for safety?
- What's the smallest version of the person I want to become that I could be
  tomorrow?

In **single-sitting** mode, walk through all nine as a contemplation block
(still offer to save them as real reminders to *live* on a future day).
In **day-spread** mode, create these as actual timed reminders (see SKILL.md
Step 4) and have the user report back as they fire.

## Part 3 — Evening: Synthesizing Insight

**Switch to active-synthesis mode here.** Help the user compress and structure
the raw material they produced in Parts 1–2. Quote their own words back.

15. After today, what feels most true about why you've been stuck?
16. What is the actual enemy? Name it clearly. Not circumstances. Not other
    people. The internal pattern or belief that has been running the show.
17. Write a single sentence that captures what you refuse to let your life become.
    This is your anti-vision compressed. It should make you feel something when
    you read it.
18. Write a single sentence that captures what you're building toward, knowing it
    will evolve. This is your vision MVP.

### Goals as lenses (not finish lines)

Frame: *"These aren't goals for the sake of achievement. Think of a goal as a
point of view — a lens you put on to enter the right state of mind to act. Don't
worry about a finish line; enjoyment is in the progress."*

19. **One-year lens:** What would have to be true in one year for you to know
    you've broken the old pattern? One concrete thing.
20. **One-month lens:** What would have to be true in one month for the one-year
    lens to remain possible?
21. **Daily lens:** What are 2-3 actions you can timeblock tomorrow that the
    person you're becoming would simply do?

## Closing — Turn Your Life Into a Video Game

Assemble everything into one coherent plan of six components, then map each to the
game metaphor. Pull the content from what the user already produced.

The six components (have these written down on one page):

1. **Anti-vision** — What is the bane of my existence, the life I never want to
   experience again?
2. **Vision** — What is the ideal life I think I want and can improve as I work
   toward it?
3. **1-year goal** — What will my life look like in 1 year, and is that closer to
   the life I want?
4. **1-month project** — What do I need to learn? What skills do I need to
   acquire? What can I build that moves me closer to the one-year goal?
5. **Daily levers** — What are the priority, needle-moving tasks that bring my
   project closer to completion?
6. **Constraints** — What am I not willing to sacrifice to achieve my vision from
   the ground up?

The game metaphor (explain it as you present the finished plan):

- **Vision** = how you win (until the game evolves).
- **Anti-vision** = what's at stake — what happens if you lose or give up.
- **1-year goal** = the mission — your sole priority.
- **1-month project** = the boss fight — how you gain XP and acquire loot.
- **Daily levers** = the quests — the daily process that unlocks new opportunity.
- **Constraints** = the rules — the limitations that force creativity.

Close on Koe's framing: these act as concentric forcefields guarding your mind
from distraction and shiny objects. The more you play, the stronger it gets,
until it's simply who you are.
