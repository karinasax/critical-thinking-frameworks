# The Method — Why This Protocol Works

Read this so you can coach with understanding rather than just reading questions
aloud. Weave it in *lightly* — a sentence of framing when the user needs to trust
the process, not a lecture. The user came for the exercise, not the theory.

## The central claim: first-order vs. second-order change

People chase goals by changing their **actions** (second-order, least important)
and hyping themselves into discipline for a few weeks — then snap back, because
they were "building a great life on a rotting foundation." Real change is
**first-order**: becoming the kind of person whose behavior *naturally* produces
the outcome. A fit person doesn't grind to eat well; they'd have to grind to eat
badly. The lifestyle that creates an outcome must exist *before* the outcome
arrives — and persist after, or the person reverts.

Coaching implication: when a user states a goal ("lose 30 lbs," "quit my job"),
the useful move isn't to plan the actions — it's to surface the *identity* that
would make those actions automatic, and the identity they'd have to give up.
That's why the protocol spends so long on identity and anti-vision before goals.

## All behavior is goal-oriented (teleology)

Every behavior is reaching for something, often unconsciously. Procrastination
isn't "lack of discipline" — it's pursuing a goal like avoiding the judgment that
comes from finishing and sharing work. Staying in a dead-end job isn't "not being
a risk-taker" — it's pursuing safety, predictability, and an excuse not to look
like a failure. The cover story (lazy, undisciplined, not-a-risk-taker) is
socially acceptable; the real goal underneath is the lever.

Coaching implication: this is exactly why you probe shallow answers *once*. When
a user gives the reasonable-sounding explanation, the real material is the
unflattering goal underneath. Question 3 ("what would someone watching your
behavior conclude you actually want?") and the self-protection questions
(10, 11) are built to expose this. Don't supply the answer — invite it.

## The identity cycle (how you became who you are)

1. You want a goal → 2. you perceive reality through that goal's lens →
3. you notice only "important" info that serves it (learning) → 4. you act and
get feedback → 5. you repeat until it's automatic (conditioning) → 6. it becomes
"I am the type of person who..." → 7. you defend that identity to stay
psychologically consistent → 8. the identity shapes new goals, and the cycle
restarts.

The leverage point is **between 6 and 7**: before the identity hardens into
something you'll defend. When identity is threatened, people go into fight-or-
flight just as with a physical threat — which is why honest reflection here is
genuinely uncomfortable and why people avoid it.

Coaching implication: name this when a user flinches or gets defensive. "That
flinch is the identity defending itself — it's a sign we're on the right thread,
not a sign to back off." Stay kind.

## Goals are lenses, not finish lines

A goal is "a projection into the future that acts as a lens of perception" — it
determines what you notice, what counts as success or failure, and how you
experience the journey. So you don't pursue goals for achievement; you *wear*
them to enter the state of mind that produces the right action. There's no finish
line; enjoyment is in the progress. This reframes the one-year/one-month/daily
"goals" in Part 3 as lenses to swap, not boxes to tick.

## The three phases of a real flip

People who successfully change their identity tend to move through:
1. **Dissonance** — fed up; they don't belong in their current life.
2. **Uncertainty** — they don't know what's next; they experiment or get lost.
3. **Discovery** — they find what to pursue and "make 6 years of progress in 6
   months."

The whole protocol is engineered to manufacture dissonance (the anti-vision),
move through uncertainty (the interrupts), and reach discovery (the synthesis).
Knowing this helps you locate where a user is and not rush them. A user still in
comfortable denial needs the dissonance questions to land before the vision work
will mean anything.

## Intelligence as steering (cybernetics)

Koe defines intelligence (via Naval) as "getting what you want out of life," and
explains it through cybernetics — "the art of steering": have a goal, act, sense
where you are, compare to the goal, act again. High intelligence = iterate,
persist, see the big picture. Low intelligence = get stuck on a problem and quit.
This underwrites the daily-lever / feedback loop in the closing plan: the plan is
a steering system, and the user is expected to course-correct, not execute
perfectly.

## A note on the ego-development stages

Koe references a 9-stage model (his "Human 3.0" synthesis of Maslow,
Loevinger/Cook-Greuter, Spiral Dynamics, Integral Theory): Impulsive →
Self-Protective → Conformist → Self-Aware → Conscientious → Individualist →
Strategist → Construct-Aware → Unitive. He assumes most readers sit between 4 and
8. You don't need to run a stage assessment to use the protocol — but it can help
you calibrate. Someone nearer stage 4 ("I feel meant for more but can't make
sense of it") needs more dissonance and permission; someone nearer 7-8 is often
reflecting analytically and needs you to keep it concrete and personal, not
abstract. Don't label the user with a stage out loud; just use it to set depth.
